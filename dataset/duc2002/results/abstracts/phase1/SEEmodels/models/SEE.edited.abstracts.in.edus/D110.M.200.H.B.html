[1] Iraq ended its 1980-1988 war with Iran in debt for about $80 billion. [2] On July 17, 1990 Iraq provoked a major crisis by accusing Kuwait of costing it billions by exceeding Kuwait's OPEC assigned oil production quotas, [3] thus driving down the price of oil, [4] by stealing Iraqi oil from a disputed border oil field, and also by demanding repayment of a $15 billion war debt. [5] After Egyptian and Saudi Arabian mediation failed, [6] Iraq invaded Kuwait with 100,000 troops led by 350 tanks. [7] It quickly overran Kuwaiti defenses [8] and continued south to the oil fields. [9] Kuwait leaders, including the emir, [10] escaped to Saudi Arabia. [11] They appealed for U.S. military intervention. [12] The Pentagon diverted an aircraft carrier group toward the gulf. [13] A U.S. led [14] U.N. resolution demanded [15] that Iraq immediately [16] and unconditionally withdraw its forces from Kuwait. [17] Iraq responded [18] that it invaded at the request of a Kuwaiti revolutionary group [19] that had already overthrown the government of Kuwait and this was not a U.N. matter. [20] The U.N. rejected this justification as a farce [21] and imposed strong economic and military sanctions [22] after Iraq failed to withdraw. [23] After a U.S. warning [24] against invading Saudi Arabia, Iraq alerted its population to a possible U.S. attack.
