[1] The winning Super Bowl team gets the Vince Lombardi Trophy [2] and is usually invited to the White House. [3] But The Super Bowl is more than a game, [4] it is big business. [5] Though tax laws governing business entertainment were tightened in 1987, [6] corporate ticket purchases are still high. [7] Approximately 100,000 people visit the host city for a week of pre-game festivities, [8] generating approximately $200 million in revenues for the community. [9] Bidding for the game is increasingly competitive. [10] In 1990, for example, the Los Angeles Sports Council, [11] hoping to lure the game to Pasadena, [12] offered the Rose Bowl rent-free for Super Bowl XXVII. [13] Television, of course, is part of the Super Bowl equation. [14] Both Miller Brewing Co. and Anheuser Bush spent over $7million on ads [15] that aired before and during Super Bowl XXIV. [16] That game, unlike the previous Super Bowl [17] that ended in a dramatic 20-16 San Francisco victory over Cincinnati, [18] was a sleeper. San Francisco routed Denver 55-10 in the lowest rated Super Bowl since 1969. [19] Frequently, the Super Bowl will highlight a community's problems. [20] For example, a parade in conjunction with the 1991 Super Bowl in Tampa was cancelled after its sponsor, an all-white, all-male club, refused to integrate.
