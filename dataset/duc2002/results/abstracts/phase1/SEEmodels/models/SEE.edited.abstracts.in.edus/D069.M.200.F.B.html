[1] Democratic reforms elsewhere in the Eastern Bloc magnified the emerging opposition to uncompromising Communist rule in East Germany. [2] The mass emigration of tens of thousands of skilled East German workers to West Germany led West German officials [3] to conclude that reunification, [4] which was codified in the West German constitution, [5] was possible. [6] Polls in West Germany supported the idea, [7] as did the U.S. President, George Bush. [8] Soviet President Gorbachev and East German leader, Erich Honecker, did not. [9] Eventually, Honecker was arrested for treason and abuse of power and Gorbachev softened his position. [10] He and interim East German Premier Hans Modrow realized [11] that the East German socialist system had failed [12] and reunion would occur. [13] However, they envisioned a gradual, no-timetable approach toward confederation [14] while European-wide issues were being worked out. [15] Of paramount importance to Gorbachev was [16] the effect reunification would have on NATO and Warsaw Pact relationships. [17] Poland was concerned [18] that a united German might want to recover 40,000 square miles of land [19] that was part of Germany before World War II. [20] And Jews worldwide feared [21] that a militarily resurgent Germany could inflict another Holocaust. [22] After Gorbachev abolished the Warsaw Pact things moved quickly. [23] All European and Jewish issues were addressed. [24] Reunification occurred.
