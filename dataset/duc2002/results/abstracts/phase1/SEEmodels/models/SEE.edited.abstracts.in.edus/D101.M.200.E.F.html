[1] Soviet hard-liners attempted a coup [2] in order to thwart the impending signing of the new Union Treaty incorporating the reforms of Soviet President Gorbachev. [3] The coup leaders put Gorbachev under house arrest in the Crimea. [4] Russian President Boris Yeltsin led opposition to the coup, [5] which did not have popular support, [6] and it was put down after three days. [7] Gorbachev was returned to Moscow [8] and was reinstated as president. [9] Some observers wondered about Gorbachev's actions [10] just before the coup attempt [11] since he had ignored ample warnings that it might happen. [12] Conspiracy buffs even wondered [13] if Gorbachev might have even been part of the planning. [14] Upon his return to power, Gorbachev ordered the arrest of the coup leaders [15] and made new appointments to key positions. [16] The putdown of the coup meant [17] that the reforms giving more authority to the republics would go forward. [18] Gorbachev and Yeltsin approached the reforms differently. [19] Gorbachev wanted to work through the Communist Party, [20] but Yeltsin had quit the party [21] and wanted to see its influence lessened. [22] Yeltsin was clearly more in touch with the people. [23] The two leaders made a joint appearance at the Russian Parliament [24] where it became increasingly clear that Gorbachev's authority was waning [25] while Yeltsin's was ascending.
