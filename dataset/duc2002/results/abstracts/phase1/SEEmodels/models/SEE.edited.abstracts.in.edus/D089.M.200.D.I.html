[1] A powerful cold front moved through the eastern half of the United States on November 15 and 16, [2] carving a deadly path of destruction. [3] The abnormally warm air preceding the front spawned tornados, violent thunderstorms, and heavy rainfall. [4] Tornados struck in at least seven states. [5] Hardest hit was Huntsville, Alabama, [6] where a tornado roared down a major street near rush hour, leveling a shopping district, apartment development, and elementary school. [7] Seventeen were killed, [8] 463 injured, [9] and 1000 left homeless. [10] Witnesses recall a huge roar, water and winds, screaming, hiding, [11] and helping others. [12] Roads were blocked [13] and power and telephone lines were cut. [14] Rescuers, including National Guard troops, worked all night [15] sifting through the rubble. [16] Damages were estimated at $100 million, with about $1 million needed to provide emergency clothing and temporary housing. [17] President Bush declared Huntsville a disaster area. [18] Marilyn Quayle, wife of the Vice President, toured the area with Alabama's governor and senators on November 18. [19] Near Newburgh, New York high winds caused an elementary school cafeteria to cave in on November 16. [20] Seven children were killed [21] and 18 injured. [22] A truck driver also was killed in New Jersey and a woman died in Illinois. [23] Injuries occurred in five other states.
