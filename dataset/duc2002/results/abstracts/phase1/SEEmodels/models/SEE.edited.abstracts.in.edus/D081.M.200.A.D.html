[1] Miners throughout the world have used strikes not only to achieve economic gains, [2] but to gain greater control over their industries and to achieve political ends. [3] Miners in Poland struck not only for higher wages, but also for the legalization of Solidarity and the reinstatement of workers [4] fired for union activity. [5] They also wanted a clear restructuring strategy for their industry. [6] Coal miners throughout the Soviet Union struck for more local control of their industries as well as better living and working conditions. [7] The labor unrest was the Soviet Union's worst in 60 years. [8] Until Gorbachev rose to power in 1985, [9] strikes were not tolerated. Miners in Kazakhstan and the Ukraine demanded real sovereignty for the 15 Soviet republics as well as a law [10] to allow private ownership of industry [11] and land. [12] Coal miners in Romania, spearheaded violent anti-reform rampages and well as demand for increased wages. [13] In South Africa coal and gold miners were to begin a strike for higher wages and working conditions. [14] Coal and gold account for more than half of South Africa's foreign currency earnings. [15] In the United States coal miners in four states walked off the job in sympathy for miners in a bitter dispute with Pittston Coal Group.
