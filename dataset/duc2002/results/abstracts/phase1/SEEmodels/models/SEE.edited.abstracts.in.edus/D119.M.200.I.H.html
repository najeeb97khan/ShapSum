[1] Bush ambassadorial nominations of veteran diplomats included James Lilley (former CIA officer) to China, [2] John Negroponte (who as ambassador to Honduras helped direct CIA aid to Nicaraguan rebels) to Mexico, [3] Deane Hinton (former ambassador to El Salvador and Costa Rica) [4] to Panama following the US invasion, Harry Shlaudeman (former ambassador to Argentina, Peru, Brazil, and Venezuela) to Nicaragua [5] (over early favorite Melissa Wells, [6] who had no Latin American experience [7] and might have faced confirmation delays), and Thomas Pickering (experienced in delicate situations concerning Iraq and El Salvador) to nuclear power India. [8] Jordan saw the appointment there of seasoned ambassador Wesley Egan as a signal of Jordan's importance to the US. [9] At the UN, Foreign Service Director Edward Perkins replaced Pickering as ambassador and Ronald Spiers got the top-ranked American post. [10] The ambassadorial nominations of Joseph Zappala (Spain), [11] Marvin Semble (Australia), [12] Della Newman (New Zealand), and Joseph Gildenhorn (Switzerland) [13] were approved despite charges [14] that their only qualifications were large campaign contributions. [15] Senate Foreign Relations Chairman Claiborne Pell called the nomination of Peter Secchia as ambassador to Italy a mistake [16] because of his big mouth. [17] Routinely approved nominations included Shirley Temple Black (Czechoslovakia), [18] Thomas Melady (Vatican), [19] Keith Brown (Denmark), and William Howard Taft IV (NATO).
