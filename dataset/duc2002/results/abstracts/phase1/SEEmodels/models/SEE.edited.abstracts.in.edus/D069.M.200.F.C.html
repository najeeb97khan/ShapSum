[1] Through the first three months of 1989, 80,00 East Germans fled their country, and massive demonstrations [2] raised doubts that Erich Honeker the, hard-line Communist leader could resist the democratic reforms sweeping his country. [3] Street demonstrations occurred throughout East Germany in November 1989, [4] and the East German Communist Party was forced to admit the its system had failed. [5] The West German constitution called for a united Germany, [6] but it received little attention until late 1989. [7] The specter of a powerful undivided Germany troubled many, but particularly the Soviets and Poles. [8] Matters moved quickly in early 1990. [9] Soviet President Gorbachev gave West Germany an unequivocal promise [10] that German reunification would be honored. [11] Two days later, on the 12th of February, said [12] that negotiations with East Germany should begin as soon as possible after the 18 March East German elections. [13] In time the World War II Allies would endorse reunification. [14] With reunification imminent, [15] world Jewish leaders asked for guarantees [16] that the Holocaust would not be repeated. [17] Most prominent American historians agreed [18] that a stable Germany was a lesser threat to stability than the woeful conditions and near chaos in East Germany.
