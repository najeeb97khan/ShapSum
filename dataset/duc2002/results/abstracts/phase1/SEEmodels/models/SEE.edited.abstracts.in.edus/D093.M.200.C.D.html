[1] Overloading is a major contributor [2] to ferry and passenger boat accidents, particularly in the third world. [3] Ferries in India, Guatemala, Egypt, China and Bangladesh all carried far more passengers [4] than authorized [5] when they sank. [6] Captain error also contributed to accidents. [7] In Guatemala, a ferry captain was charges with negligent homicide [8] when his boat which was being towed, sank after he failed to steer it. [9] A ferry sank off the Egyptian coast after inexplicably leaving standard navigational channels [10] and ramming a coral reef in stormy seas. [11] In that accident, survivors claimed [12] that crewmembers abandoned ship without helping them. [13] The ship's engineer denied the charges. [14] Two boats carrying Somali refugees sank off the coast of Kenya, [15] resulting in more than 140 deaths, [16] although the cause of the sinking was not reported. [17] An overcrowded ferry in Bangladesh sank with almost double the passengers [18] it was authorized. [19] In a separate ferry incident in Bangladesh, pirates robbed passengers [20] and threw six overboard. [21] In the Indian accident, monsoon rains may have contributed as well as overcrowding. [22] Even an advanced country such as Israel can experience ferry accidents. [23] Nineteen U.S. sailors died [24] when an Israeli ferry sank [25] while returning the sailors to the USS Saratoga. [26] No cause was reported.
