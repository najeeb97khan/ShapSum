[1] In early May elections across Britain, the Conservatives suffered their worst defeat ever, [2] losing 444 seats. [3] One editorial claims [4] some Conservatives will link the defeats to Prime Minister John Major, [5] and there is speculation [6] that he could be forced out this autumn. [7] A former welfare recipient, John Major was elected Conservative Party leader and successor to his mentor Margaret Thatcher [8] when she resigned under pressure in November 1990. [9] After Mrs. Thatcher submitted her seals of office, [10] Major was called by Queen Elizabeth II [11] to form a new government, [12] and it was expected [13] that there would be no significant policy shift. [14] Major's comment [15] while visiting America a month later that Iraqi troops might have to be "forcibly removed" from Kuwait echoed Mrs. Thatcher's hard- line on the Persian Gulf. [16] Within a year of winning the general election, [17] his popularity had plunged. [18] In the July 1993 Christchurch by-election the Conservative majority of 23,015 [19] the previous year was converted into a 16,427 majority for the Liberal Democrats. [20] Though Major's personal style is preferable to that of Mrs. Thatcher, he is characterized as a vacillating, weak leader, [21] lacking vision and political conviction. [22] Not even recent revelations [23] that Major uses salty language can alter his wimp image.
