[1] On Tuesday, April 24, 1990, shuttle Discovery carried the 12.5-ton Hubble Space Telescope 380 miles into space. [2] Once in orbit above Earth's distorting atmosphere, [3] Hubble would be able to see 10 times better than the largest Earth telescopes. [4] Wednesday it was lifted into space by Discovery's mechanical arm [5] and released into orbit. [6] Friday the 94.5-inch mirror was exposed to starlight. [7] Saturday controllers took Hubble out of safe mode. [8] Problems with a solar power array, communications antennas, and two position- stabilizing gyroscopes [9] - exacerbated by conservative limits set on systems - [10] put Hubble several days behind schedule [11] and delayed transmittal of the first photograph. [12] Discovery's astronauts - Shriver, Bolden, Hawley, McCandless, and Sullivan [13] - - were all veterans of previous flights. [14] The shuttle followed Hubble for two days [15] to assist if necessary. [16] A seven- month checkout period would follow. [17] Spacelab Astro was to be put up two weeks later to give Hubble additional target information. [18] Hubble, delayed since 1983, cost $1.5 billion. [19] Spare parts, training, and software cost $500 million more. [20] Maintenance cost $7 million a month on Earth [21] and will cost $200 million yearly in orbit, [22] for Hubble's 15-year lifetime. [23] Discovery carried the Hooker Telescope eyepiece used by astronomer Edwin Hubble, [24] for whom the Hubble is named.
