[1] Wal-Mart founder, Sam Walton, [2] who died in April 1992, [3] revolutionized discount retailing. [4] Born in Oklahoma, the son of a mortgage banker, [5] he became the richest man in America [6] and the second richest in the world. [7] After a tour in the army [8] and working in and owning retailing outlets, [9] he founded Wal-Mart Discount City in 1962. [10] He took discount shopping to rural America [11] and by 1992 Wal-Mart was the world's largest retailer. [12] Success can be attributed to Sam's personality. [13] He was frugal, hardworking, old- fashioned, and homespun. [14] He declared the customer always right and continually nurtured employee satisfaction with bonus payments, profit-sharing plans, and a responsive, streamlined, management system. [15] He was known for pop-in visits, [16] talking with customers, [17] and personally helping out in an emergency. [18] Sam Walton also worked to preserve his personal legacy. [19] In 1990 the Wal-Mart Stores Inc., Visitors Center opened to chronicle the rise of the chain. [20] He also wrote his autobiography, [21] filling it with anecdotes about his "good ole" common sense. [22] A scholarly book was released in 1994, [23] which highlighted his ability to copy and adapt [24] and addressed some of the negative aspects of the giant chain, including its anti-union stand and its impact on small town Mom-and-Pop stores.
