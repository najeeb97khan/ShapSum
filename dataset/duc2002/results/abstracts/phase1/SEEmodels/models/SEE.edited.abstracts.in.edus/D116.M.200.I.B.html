[1] Following an aborted first try, [2] the shuttle Discovery soared into space [3] to launch its history-making payload, the $1.5 billion Hubble Space Telescope. [4] Named [5] after the American astronomer Edwin Hubble [6] who did pioneering work [7] leading to the "Big Bang" concept of the universe, [8] it is NASA's most expensive spacecraft. [9] While orbiting at 380 statute miles above Earth during its expected 15-year working lifetime, [10] it will provide astronomers world-wide with 10 times better resolution and 25 times more sensitivity than ground-based observatories. [11] Free of Earth's distorting atmosphere, it may be able to look back almost to the beginning of time. [12] The telescope's release from the shuttle Discovery came two hours and one orbit later than planned. [13] A troublesome solar power panel took three tries before it fully unfurled, causing the delay. [14] Two Mission Specialists astronauts were standing-by to unfurl the panel manually if necessary. [15] Then more start-up problems, ground controllers were unable to link up the telescope's two high-gain antennas with their assigned relay satellite. [16] A weaker back-up antenna system is working. [17] The telescope was placed on special flight modes [18] until these start-up problems are resolved. [19] Confident that the Hubble will soon be operational, [20] the shuttle Discovery is preparing to return home.
