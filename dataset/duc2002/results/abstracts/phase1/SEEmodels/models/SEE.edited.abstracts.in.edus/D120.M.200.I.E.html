[1] John Major was 47 when he was elected to lead the Conservative Party. [2] Assuming the position of prime Minister from Margaret, [3] Mr. Major became the youngest prime minister since the 19th century. [4] Mr. Major was confirmed as leader of the Conservative Party [5] when his two opponents, former Defense Minister Michael Heseltine and Foreign Secretary Douglas Hurd, conceded defeat in an election [6] which saw Major two votes shy of a majority. [7] Major, whose father was a trapeze artist in the circus, [8] overcame many obstacles in his rise to the top. [9] He was a high school drop out [10] and for a time was a welfare recipient. [11] Mr. Major had little time to enjoy his victory as public opinion of him soon soured. [12] While seen as gentler than his abrasive predecessor, [13] he is viewed as timid and indecisive. [14] Almost from the first he has been beset by scandal in his cabinet and a number of controversies ranging from internal party differences over Europe to the debacle surrounding Britain's withdrawal from the exchange rates mechanism of the European Monetary System to a deep and continuing recession. [15] Major came out in strong support of U.S. policy in the Persian Gulf, [16] calling on Iraqi President Saddam Hussein to withdraw his troops from Kuwait.
