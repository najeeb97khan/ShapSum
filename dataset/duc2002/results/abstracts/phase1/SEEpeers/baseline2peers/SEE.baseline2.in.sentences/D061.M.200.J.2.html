[1] Hurricane Gilbert's growth from a harmless low pressure zone off Africa to a ferocious killer in the Gulf of Mexico was fueled by a combination of heat, moisture and wind that baffles forecasters. [2] ``It's a matter of getting everything together in the right place in the right time,'' Gil Clark of the National Hurricane Center said Thursday. [3] ``It doesn't happen very often. [4] How it develops, we don't know''. [5] Gilbert came to the attention of center forecasters Sept. 3 as a dry low pressure trough moving west out of Africa. [6] ``We get 50 or 60 of these off Africa every summer. [7] About one of six develop,'' said Clark. [8] By Sept. 8, the system became a depression. [9] It reached tropical storm status by Saturday and a hurricane Sunday. [10] A tropical wave becomes a depression when winds start swirling. [11] When sustained winds reach 39 mph, the system becomes a named tropical storm. [12] It reaches hurricane status when sustained winds hit 74 mph. [13] Why Gilbert organized and strengthened while other systems didn't ``is a mystery more or less,'' said University of Miami meteorology Professor Rainer Bleck. [14] ``The first part of the summer we were biting our nails, wondering why these (other) disturbances didn't develop,'' he said Thursday.
