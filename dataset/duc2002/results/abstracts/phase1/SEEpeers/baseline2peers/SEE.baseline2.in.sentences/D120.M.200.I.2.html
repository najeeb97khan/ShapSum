[1] Last year a biography of Mrs Norma Major - or, if we are being old-fashioned, Mrs John Major - claimed that the Majors' favourite film was Being There. [2] I entirely concur with their choice. [3] In case you did not see that marvellous film, this, in essence, was the plot: a man no one knows anything about, a gardener called Chauncey, rises without trace to become, by the end of the film, a serious candidate for the US presidency. [4] Chauncey, played by Peter Sellers, utters only mundane sentiments in a halting monotone but this convinces the Republican party's power brokers that he is a sure-fire winner. [5] In Chauncey's bromides everyone seems to hear what they want to believe. [6] He has a deadly innocence. [7] No wonder the Majors liked this film. [8] John Major must, at some level, have appreciated the similarity between his own, strangely intangible, rise and that of Chauncey's. [9] If he did not see the similarity, that is still more revealing. [10] Do not protest that the urban John Major could not identify with a gardener. [11] In an interview with Farmers Weekly, a few days ago, Major disclosed an unknown fact: his father was once a farmer. [12] Farmers Weekly, you see, had asked Major how he had 'such obvious empathy with agriculture'.
